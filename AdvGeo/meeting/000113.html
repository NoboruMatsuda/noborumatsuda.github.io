Meeting with Marsha Lovett at CMU  (lovett@andrew.cmu.edu)
January 13, 2000  1:30pm - 2:30pm

  • In our discussion, ...
    • "diagram" (in Marsha's paper) is a topological structure of a theorem to prove.  This also refers to a composition of specific configurations (as opposed to an abstract configuration described below).
    • "configuration" (in our paper) is a topological structure of an axiom used to prove theorems.  This refers to an abstract entity (e.g., one can imagine an arbitrary triangle for the isosceles-triangle axiom).
  • Behavioral change though learning in geometry  (abstract -> specific -> abstract)
    1. Students begin to learn geometry with axioms, which are described with configurations (i.e., in an abstracted form).
       .
    2. To learn how to use axioms (i.e., problem-solving strategy), they need to be fed bunch of problems.  In other words, they must struggle with specific diagrams for a while. 

      In this stage, diagram might be a basis of students' memory of proof. (Marsha et al. 1994)  They tend to use previous experience.

      However, since learned axioms are not abstracted enough, they suffer from the retroactive interference effects; solving two problems with the same diagram but different logic cause a difficulty to solve the third problem.
       .
    3. Eventually, they become not to rely on the diagrams in their past experience, but to be able to retrieve axioms from their memory. (Koedinger et al. 1990)
    • So, both diagram and configuration play a central role in learning geometry.
  • To see if the above hypothesis is true, we can test experts in geometry with the same paradigm as the one in Marsha's work; since they have fairly abstracted axioms, they should not suffer from the retroactive interference effects.
  • Design issues ...
    • Clicking an icon for forward chaining might confuse students, because several axioms are applied at the same time.  They do not think all the applicable axioms at the same time.
    • Students might want to know how a particular fact (in the facts list) is deduced.
    • Lining up the icons for forward chaining and construction, and backward chaining at the same place might confuse students, because the former stand for set of axioms, while the latter represents an axiom.  They might reason in terms of axiom.
  • Experiment issues ...
    • Conduct experiments with and without DS icons to see how the reification with DS icons works.
    • If you compare our GUI with ANGLE (Koedinger et al.), you have to be aware of following factors on learning effects.
      • solution tree vs search tree
      • displaying a whole tree vs 1-by-1 presentation
      • DC model vs GRAMY (a model of problem solving)
