Floundering vs. Exploring

Floundering represents student's irrelevant, erroneous, frustrated actions that must be got rid of.  The student might make an error, which in turn trap the student in an impasse. 

Exploring represents student's efforts to achieve a goal.  Must be encouraged (true?). 

 

The protocol data on Geometry show evidence that students need to explore.

They are not following a certain solution path, which is expected by most MMT. 

 

A lesson learning from Andes:

Students do flounder a lot even when they get enormous amount of help.

Why?  Nobody knows...

The students might not know backward chaining, hence a global help doesn't make much sense to them.

The students might not know domain principles.

 

What they have learned from Andes:

Old Andes

Huge amount of protocol with Ss actions synchronized with their utterance.

Most students floundered a lot and very frustrated.

Snapshot critique

Confirmed a need both for local and global help.

Local help:

Along with the student's solution path, give a immediate feedback to put the student back onto right track. 

Global help:

When got stuck, give a top down (backward chaining) help to start over the solution.

Andes II

Implemented a global help provided when student ask "what's next."

Ss actually ask "what's next" help a lot, but they still floundered. 

 

A challenging problem:

How to maximize student's exploration while minimizing their floundering.

 

So, the research question might be about student's initiative in exploratory learning.

Andes is indeed a version of exploratory model tracer!

 

We would compare different initiative control strategies to figure out how to control student vs. tutor initiative to obtain maximum learning gain. 

Prospective control conditions:

(1) wide open:  no tutoring system at all.  Ss work with paper and pencil.  Maximum exploration, maximum floundering.

(2) Scaffolding:  provide as much scaffolding as possible to prevent students from floundering.  Minimum exploration, minimum floundering. 

Possible threat:  pacing.

If you let students go through with very slow speed, then they would learn things well anyway.  Slow pacing would wiped out the truth. 

 

Stop floundering:

Andes III is supposed to struggle with the floundering by (1) reifying the whole problem solving process, (2) cutting down memory load, and (3) providing well organized curriculum. 

 

Next step:

Finish up the data analysis. 

Make a comparison between exploration and floundering.

Hopefully, well formalized so that a machine can determine, from activity log, if a student is exploring or floundering. 

Visualization of student's reasoning steps would be a plus.
